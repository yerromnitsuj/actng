# odp-bootstrap-england-verrall-shapland — primary-source transcription (research workflow, 2026-07-17)

## formulation

ALL FROM FETCHED PRIMARY SOURCES: England & Verrall (2002) BAJ 8 "Stochastic Claims Reserving in General Insurance" [E&V02, PDF: actuaries.org.uk/system/files/documents/pdf/sm0201.pdf]; England (2002) "Addendum to 'Analytic and bootstrap estimates of prediction errors in claims reserving'", IME 31:461-466 [Eng02, PDF: www1feb-uva.nl/ke/act/people/kaas/Eng02.pdf] which restates and extends E&V (1999) IME 25:281-293; Shapland, CAS Monograph No. 4 (2016) [Shp16, PDF: casact.org/sites/default/files/2021-03/7_Shapland.pdf].

(a) THE ODP CROSS-CLASSIFIED GLM AND CL EQUIVALENCE. E&V02 §2.3: incremental claims C_ij (i=row/origin, j=delay) are independent over-dispersed Poisson with E[C_ij] = m_ij = x_i·y_j and Var[C_ij] = φ·x_i·y_j, with Σ_{k=1..n} y_k = 1 (x_i = expected ultimate for row i, y_j = proportion emerging in delay j, φ = unknown over-dispersion/scale parameter estimated from the data). For estimation it is re-parameterised as a log-link GLM: log(m_ij) = c + α_i + β_j with corner constraints α_1 = β_1 = 0 (§2.3.4). Quasi-likelihood fitting means data need not be positive integers; the crucial assumption is variance ∝ mean (§2.3.5). Since y_j appears in the variance, y_j must be positive: individual negative incrementals are allowed but no development-column sum may be negative (§2.3.2). Equivalence: "the reserve estimates are identical to the chain-ladder results" (E&V02 ¶7.2.14; model due to Renshaw & Verrall 1998); Shp16 §3.4: "it is ONLY when we specify a parameter for EVERY accident year and EVERY development year and specify a Poisson error distribution that we end up exactly replicating the volume weighted average development factors", which lets the GLM fit be replaced by the deterministic development-factor algorithm ("simplified GLM" / "ODP bootstrap", Shp16 §3.2). PRACTICAL COMPUTATION OF FITTED m̂_ij (E&V02 Appendix 3, A3.1, verbatim step list): (1) compute standard volume-weighted CL development factors λ̂_j = Σ_{i=1..n−j+1} D_ij / Σ_{i=1..n−j+1} D_{i,j−1} from cumulative data D_ij = Σ_{k≤j} C_ik; (2) obtain cumulative FITTED values for the PAST triangle by BACKWARDS RECURSION starting from the observed latest diagonal: D̂_{i,n−i+1} = D_{i,n−i+1}, then D̂_{i,k−1} = D̂_{i,k}·λ̂_k^{−1}; (3) obtain incremental fitted values m̂_ij for the past triangle by differencing each row. (Same steps in Eng02 §2 and Shp16 §3.2: "reproduced by using the last cumulative diagonal, dividing backwards successively by each volume-weighted average development factor and subtracting".)

(b) RESIDUALS. Unscaled Pearson residuals on INCREMENTAL data: r^(P)_ij = (C_ij − m̂_ij)/√m̂_ij (E&V02 eq. A3.1 and eq. 8.1; Eng02 eq. 2.4; Shp16 eq. 3.16 with variance power z=1, i.e., r = (q(w,d) − m_{w,d})/√(m_{w,d}^z)). "Unscaled" = φ omitted; φ is not needed for the resampling but is needed for process error (Eng02 §2).

(c) DEGREES OF FREEDOM AND BIAS CORRECTION. Eng02 eq. 3.1: replace r_P by r′_P = sqrt(n/(n−p))·(C−m)/√m, "where n is the number of data points in the sample, p the number of parameters estimated" — applied to the residuals BEFORE resampling so the correction flows through to the predictive distribution automatically ("inflating the variance while leaving the mean largely unchanged"). For an I×I triangle in E&V's parameterisation: n = I(I+1)/2 observations and p = 2I−1 parameters (constant + (I−1) α's + (I−1) β's), so n−p = ½I(I+1) − 2I + 1; for I=10: n=55, p=19, n−p=36, factor sqrt(55/36) ≈ 1.2360. CAUTION: E&V02 Appendix 3 prints the adjustment as sqrt(n/(½n(n+1)−2n+1)) with an overloaded "n" — there n in the DENOMINATOR expression is the triangle dimension while the NUMERATOR n is the number of observations (½n(n+1)); Eng02 eq. 3.1 is the unambiguous statement. Shapland (Shp16 eq. 3.19): f^DoF = sqrt(N/(N−p)), N = "number of observations, or incremental data cells in the triangle, which is typically equal to n×(n+1)÷2", and the monograph text prints "p = the number of parameters, which is typically equal to 2×(n−1)" — NOTE this printed p=2(n−1) (=18 for 10×10, DoF 37) conflicts with E&V's p=2n−1 (=19, DoF 36) and with Shapland's own 3×3 worked GLM which solves for 5 = 2n−1 parameters (eq. 3.13: α1,α2,α3,β2,β3); treat p = 2I−1 as the E&V-consistent choice and flag the monograph's 2(n−1) in a doc comment. Scaled residuals r^S = r·f^DoF (Shp16 eq. 3.20). Shapland further notes (per Pinheiro et al. 2001/2003) that f^DoF does not standardize residuals; the improvement is the hat-matrix factor f^H_{w,d} = sqrt(1/(1−H_ii)) with H = X(XᵀWX)⁻¹XᵀW (eqs. 3.21–3.24), "a replacement for, and an improvement on, the degrees of freedom factor (3.19)".

(d) SCALE PARAMETER. φ = Σ(r^(P)_ij)²/(n−p), i.e., sum of squared UNSCALED Pearson residuals over the past triangle divided by degrees of freedom (E&V02 A3.1: denominator ½n(n+1)−2n+1; Eng02 eq. 2.5: φ_P = Σr²_P/(n−p); Shp16 eq. 3.17: φ = Σr²_{w,d}/(N−p)). With hat-matrix-standardized residuals the alternative is φ^H = Σ(r^H)²/N (Shp16 eq. 3.25).

(e) RESAMPLING LOOP (E&V02 Appendix 3 A3.1, verbatim steps; identical in Eng02 §2): Begin iterative loop repeated N times (N = 1000, say): (i) resample the ADJUSTED residuals with replacement, creating a new past triangle of residuals; (ii) for each past cell invert the residual definition: C* = r*·√m̂_ij + m̂_ij (pseudo-incremental data; Shp16 eq. 3.18: q*(w,d) = r*·√(m^z_{w,d}) + m_{w,d}); (iii) cumulate to pseudo-cumulative data; (iv) fit the standard chain ladder to the pseudo-cumulative data (identical reserve estimates to refitting the ODP GLM — E&V02 ¶8.2.7: "Strictly, we ought to fit an over-dispersed Poisson GLM... However, we can obtain identical reserve estimates using standard chain-ladder methodology"); (v) project to form a future triangle of cumulative payments; (vi) difference to get the future incremental means m̃_ij; (vii) PROCESS VARIANCE: for each future cell (i,j) simulate a payment from the process distribution with mean m̃_ij and variance φ·m̃_ij using the φ from step (d) (¶8.2.8: "simulating an observed claims payment for each future cell... using the bootstrap value as the mean, and using the process distribution assumed in the underlying model... over-dispersed Poisson"); (viii) sum simulated future payments by origin year and overall to get that iteration's reserves; (ix) store and repeat. GAMMA APPROXIMATION: there is no standard way to simulate ODP (Eng02 §3); options given are (1) Poisson(m/φ)×φ (values are multiples of φ), (2) "a pragmatic alternative is to sample from a negative binomial or Gamma distribution parameterised such that the mean is m and the variance is φm" (Eng02 §3 — this Gamma(mean m, variance φm) is the commonly used approximation; Shp16 §3.2 fn 17: "The Poisson distribution could be used to remain more consistent with the underlying theory... but it is considerably slower to simulate, so gamma is a close substitute that performs much faster in simulation although it can be more skewed than the Poisson"), (3) adaptive rejection sampling from the quasi-log-likelihood. In shape/rate terms Gamma(shape = m/φ, scale = φ).

(f) WHAT THE OUTPUT ESTIMATES (E&V02 A3.2; Eng02 §2-3): the stored results form the (simulated) PREDICTIVE DISTRIBUTION of reserves by origin year and in total. "The mean of the stored results should be compared to the standard chain ladder reserve estimates to check for bias" (should be reassuringly close to CL); "The standard deviation of the stored results gives an estimate of the prediction error" — i.e., the root mean squared error of prediction, combining estimation (parameter) variance and process variance, consistent with the analytic MSEP formulae (E&V02 eqs. 7.7-7.9: MSEP[Ĉ_ij] ≈ φm̂_ij + m̂²_ij Var[η̂_ij] plus covariance terms for row/total aggregation). Historical note: E&V (1999) itself bootstrapped only the ESTIMATION error and added process variance analytically: PE_bs(R) = sqrt(φ_P·R + (n/(n−p))·SE_bs(R)²) (Eng02 eq. 2.6); the two-stage bootstrap-plus-process-simulation that yields the full predictive distribution is England (2002)/E&V02 §8.2.

## caveats

ZERO/NEGATIVE INCREMENTALS (Shapland §4.1, E&V02 §2.3.2): the ODP GLM breaks when any development-column SUM is negative (y_j must be positive; individual negative cells are fine — E&V02 ¶2.3.2, ¶2.3.5 quasi-likelihood). Shapland's fixes: (1) modified log-link, eq. 4.1: ln[q] for q>0, 0 for q=0, −ln[abs(q)] for q<0; (2) if entire column sums are negative, shift by the largest negative ψ: q⁺ = q − ψ, fit, then un-shift fitted values m = m⁺ + ψ (eqs. 4.2-4.3); (3) in the resampling itself use abs under the root: r = (q − m)/sqrt(abs(m^z)) and q* = r*·sqrt(abs(m^z)) + m (eqs. 4.4-4.5) since development factors < 1 create negative fitted incrementals; (4) negative means at the PROCESS-variance step (§4.1.1): either −Gamma[abs(m), φ·abs(m)] (eq. 4.6, skews left) or Gamma[abs(m), φ·abs(m)] + 2m (eq. 4.7, keeps right skew — England 2002 §3 gives the same trick: "simulate an observation from a distribution with mean abs(m), then subtract 2×m"); (5) negative incrementals in early development can produce extreme iterations (near-zero column sums → explosive factors) — options: delete-and-replace those iterations, recalibrate the model, or floor incrementals at zero/some minimum (§4.1.1, fn 33).

ZERO-VALUE CORNER RESIDUALS: the two corner cells' residuals are identically zero due to corner constraints. England (2002) §3 keeps them in the resampling pool "to ensure that the prediction errors... are analogous to those calculated analytically" (excluding them would inflate the SD); Shapland/Pinheiro exclude zero residuals from sampling and account for them via the hat-matrix factor (Shp16 §3.2 fn 19; §4.10 notes a 10×10 triangle = 55 data points but 53 usable residuals). Pick one and document it — it changes the fixture.

CONDITIONAL vs UNCONDITIONAL: none of the three primary sources uses this terminology (verified by full-text search of the Shapland monograph — zero occurrences). The E&V/Shapland algorithm as published computes each iteration's reserve ENTIRELY from the pseudo-triangle (pseudo development factors applied to the pseudo-cumulative latest diagonal) — the variant later literature (e.g., Björkwall et al. 2009) calls "unconditional"; the "conditional" variant applies the resampled factors to the ACTUAL observed diagonal, anchoring each iteration's reserve to real paid-to-date and typically shrinking dispersion for mature years. Do not confuse this with England (2002)'s different use of "conditional": the process draw is made "conditional on the bootstrap value" (the simulated mean m̃), which is just the two-stage predictive structure. If you implement the published algorithm, say "unconditional-style, per E&V 2002 Appendix 3" in the doc comment.

RESIDUAL-ADJUSTMENT OPTIONS worth doc comments (Shapland): (1) f^DoF = sqrt(N/(N−p)) (eq. 3.19) vs hat-matrix standardization f^H = sqrt(1/(1−H_ii)) (eqs. 3.21-3.24) — the latter is "a replacement for, and an improvement on" the former; (2) the printed p: Shapland says "typically 2×(n−1)" while E&V's parameterisation implies 2n−1 (n−p = 36 for 10×10 per E&V's own φ denominator) — document which you use; (3) non-zero residual mean: resampling from residuals with positive mean biases pseudo-reserves upward; option to shift all non-zero residuals by a constant to zero mean, but a non-zero mean "could be argued... is a characteristic of the data set" (§4.2); (4) heteroscedasticity (§4.6): stratified sampling by development-period groups; hetero-adjustment factors h_i = stdev(group)/stdev(all) multiplied onto residuals and divided back out at resampling (eqs. 4.8-4.10); or per-group scale parameters φ_i with h_i = sqrt(φ)/sqrt(φ_i) (eqs. 4.11-4.13) — all add parameters, which changes N−p and hence φ and f^DoF; (5) England (2002) §5: the whole method assumes CONSTANT φ; heteroskedasticity requires joint modelling, the dispersion enters the residual definition, and fitted values may then no longer equal the deterministic CL. Other practical knobs in Shapland §4: L-year weighted average factors (use only last L+1 diagonals; sample residuals only from those diagonals for the GLM bootstrap, §4.3), missing values (§4.4), outlier exclusion from factors and residuals while still resampling into those cells (§4.5), heteroecthesious data (partial first development period / partial last calendar diagonal: annualize, then de-annualize and halve last-AY future exposure, §4.7), exposure adjustment (divide by AY exposures, restate after process variance, §4.8), stochastic tail factors (rule of thumb: tail-factor SD ≤ 50% of (tail−1), §4.9), and parametric bootstrapping (fit a distribution to the residuals and sample from it when the 53-residual pool lacks extremes, §4.10).
